Boston wasn't always this way.

In fact, the shoreline of Boston experienced continuous change over the past three centuries.

Let's take look at how Boston has transformed in such short amount of time!

1790s Boston: Early Roots

Population: 18,000

In the 1790s, Boston, then a growing town on the Shawmut Peninsula, began its early land-making efforts to expand its limited area. Initial projects like filling Mill Pond were modest but laid the foundation for future expansions that would dramatically change the city's landscape.

1830s Boston: Expanding Boundaries

Population: 61,000

By the 1830s, Boston's population had increased to about 61,000. The city's physical expansion continued as land-making projects progressed, transforming the city's geography. Notably, the Mill Pond area was completely filled by 1828, and attention turned to the South Cove, which was filled to create new land for the burgeoning city. This period witnessed the transformation of Boston's coastline, with the filled lands soon hosting residential buildings and commercial enterprises, aiding in the city's economic growth.

1860s Boston: The Industrial Push

Population: 180,000

The population soared to 180,000 by the 1860s, driven by industrialization and the influx of immigrants. The land-making efforts kept pace, with major projects like the filling of the Back Bay starting in 1857. This massive undertaking was critical in creating the now prestigious Back Bay neighborhood, drastically increasing Boston's land mass and property value. By the end of this period, Boston had not only expanded its land but had also laid down the infrastructural foundation for modern urban development.

1910s Boston: A City Transformed

Population: 670,000

By the 1910s, Boston's population had reached 670,000, and the city's skyline began to take on a more contemporary form. The completion of the Back Bay and Fenway land-making projects had created significant new residential and commercial areas. This era marked the culmination of Boston's most intensive period of land filling, which saw the city's geography and real estate market transformed. The development of these new areas helped accommodate the growing population and facilitated economic activities that leveraged the newly available space.

2020s Boston: The Present

Population 670,000

Today Boston's thriving city life serves as a testament to the city's sustained appeal and economic stability. The land-making has long been completed, but the city continues to face challenges related to its historic growth. However, environmental and infrastructural issues persist, the consequences of the long-term impacts of altering natural landscapes. Modern Boston, with its mix of historic sites and modern infrastructure, continues to navigate the complexities of urban development while maintaining its status as a major metropolitan hub.




What was Boston like?

Let's explore a map of Boston from the year 1640. At that time, much of Boston coastline consisted of marshland. These tidal areas were naturally prone to flooding. The marshy conditions were ideal for oysters, which is why Boston became famous for them.

What is the impact of this land-making on Boston?

The "View of Boston Harbor" drawing by Haberman from 1770 highlights muddy areas as darker patches, indicating mud lands. This terrain made it challenging for boats to approach the shoreline, and houses were not built there due to frequent flooding and instability.

What about flood insurance?

In 1968, Congress created the National Flood Insurance Program (NFIP). This program offered homeowners affordable flood insurance because private companies were unwilling to take on the risk of damage caused by floods. The program is administered by the Federal Emergency Management Agency (FEMA).


Rates vary depending on:
  • The flood zone your property is located in
  • Its height above sea level
  • Your building’s characteristics

    What does this mean for home‑seekers?


    To better understand the risks faced by prospective renters and homeowners in the Boston market, we compiled geospatial data from third‑party sources to overlay onto residential sales data characterizing the Boston Market between 2000 and 2023.





    With the prospect of looming sea level rise and heightened flood risk throughout Boston, we expect that the buying and selling of properties would account for this near term risk.

    However, despite overall property sales remaining consistent, we note that two categories Special Flood Hazard Area and 9" Sea Level Rise are actually increasing in the last four years.

    Similarly, we see that these zones are where more new properties are being built.




    Has property speculation not been accounting for sea‑level rise?

    It's well known that buying and selling property is a lucrative practice, but increasingly, flipping means shorter timespans between sales.

    By counting which unique addresses are most frequently sold, we can analyze the impact of this property speculation within different spatial risk zones.

    Surprisingly, we find that across categories, spatial risk has little to no correlation with how frequently a property is sold. Flood risk has not been driving market behavior.



    The properties most prone to near‑term flood risk are those we associate with waterfront communities such as North End, East Boston, and Seaport.

    As these properties are highly sought for their locations, their prices are higher on average. A diagonal clustering appears between the $1–5M range. A smaller cluster appears among properties sold 6+ times priced below $1M—potentially dangerous for new buyers.




    All property types face some exposure to flood risk in the coming century, but condominiums and apartments in particular face heightened risk. By 2070 (36" SLR), more than 20% of condos and 15% of apartments will coincide with the 100‑year flood zone.
